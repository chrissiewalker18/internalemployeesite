Natalie's "Pumpkin Gooey Butter Cake"

Pumpkin pie not your thing? Well, let them eat cake! This recipe is nothing if not a product relaunch for pumpkin pie. Your guests won't believe what they've been missing, but they'll never turn back.

Buzz words: a twist on a classic, indulgent, decadent

Ingredients:

Cake

  • 1 (18 1/4-ounce) package yellow cake mix
  • 1 egg
  • 8 tbsp butter, melted

Filling

  • 1 (8-ounce) package cream cheese, softened
  • 1 (15-ounce) can pumpkin
  • 3 eggs
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 8 tbsp butter, melted
  • 1 16-oz box powdered sugar
  • 1 tsp cinnamon
  • 1 tsp nutmeg

Preparation:

  1. Preheat oven to 350 degrees F
  2. Combine the cake mix, egg, and butter and mix well with an electric mixer. Pat the mixture into the bottom of a lightly greased 13 by 9-inch baking pan.
  3. To make the filling: In a large bowl, beat the cream cheese and pumpkin until smooth. Add the eggs, vanilla, and butter, and beat together. Next, add the powdered sugar, cinnamon, nutmeg, and mix well. Spread pumpkin mixture over cake batter and bake for 40 to 50 minutes. Make sure not to overbake as the center should be a little gooey.
  4. Serve with fresh whipped cream.

Sarah's "Maple Roasted Brussel Sprouts"

Whoever said the turkey would be the most coveted dish at Thanksgiving dinner clearly never had these brussel sprouts. Not to mention, they're unbelievably easy to make.

Buzz words: simple, addicting, sweet & savory

Ingredients:

  • 2 1/4 lbs. baby brussel sprouts or regular size halved lengthwise
  • 1/4 cup canola oil
  • salt and freshly ground pepper
  • 1 stick (4 oz) unsalted butter, cut into tablespoons and softened
  • 2 tbsp light brown sugar
  • 1/4 cup grade A pure maple syrup
  • 1 1/4 tbsp cider vinegar
  • 1 cup vacuum-packed roasted chestnuts, coarsley chopped
  • 1 tbsp walnut oil

Preparation:

  1. Heat the canola oil in a very large skillet until shimmering.
  2. Add the brussels sprouts and season with salt and pepper, then cook over high heat without stirring until they are browned (about 2 minutes)
  3. Add the unsalted butter and brown sugar and cook over moderately high heat, stirring occasionally, until the brown sugar is melted
  4. Add the maple syrup and cook, stirring occasionally, until the brussels sprouts are just crisp-tender (about 7 minutes)
  5. Stir in the cider vinegar
  6. Add the chestnuts and walnut oil and cook until hot
Sean A.’s "Lemon Truffle Pie"

This recipe is an upsell for Sean’s “Cream Cheese Blueberry Pie” from last year’s menu. Give your members a little variety with this citrusy, decadent pie! (It won’t take much convincing.)

Buzz words: sweet, refreshing, unique

Ingredients:

Crust

  • Pastry (purchased or made from scratch) for one-crust 9” pie

Filling

  • 1 cup sugar
  • 2 tbsp corn starch
  • 2 tbsp all purpose flour
  • 1 cup water
  • 2 egg yolks beaten (room temp.) in a separate bowl
  • 1 tbsp butter (room temp.)
  • Peel of one lemon, grated
  • 1/4 cup lemon juice (room temp.)
  • 1 cup vanilla chips or 6 oz. pkg. of white baking bars, chopped
  • 8 oz. light cream cheese, softened (room temp.)

Garnish

  • 1/2 cup whipping cream, beaten to soft peaks
  • 1/4 cup sliced, toasted almonds
  • Fresh berries (raspberries, blueberries, or strawberries)

Preparation:

  1. Bake and cool pie crust (if making it from scratch)
  2. In medium non-reactive pan stir together sugar, cornstarch, and flour. Gradually stir in water until smooth.
  3. Stirring constantly, cook mixture over medium heat until it boils. Continue stirring, but reduce heat to just a gentle boil, and boil for 2 to 3 minutes until mixture is thickens and clears.
  4. Remove mixture from heat. While beating the egg yolks in a separate bowl, gradually drizzle some of the hot mixture (2 to 3 Tbsp) into the eggs. Then, stirring constantly, gradually pour the bowl of egg yolks into the hot mixture. Return mixture to low heat, stirring constantly, and bring to a boil again, and boil for 2 minutes.
  5. Remove from heat, and stir in butter, lemon peel, and juice.
  6. Place 1/3 cup of hot mixture in separate pan, and stir in vanilla chips until they melt. If they do not completely melt, stir over low heat for a bit.
  7. In another small bowl, beat cream cheese until fluffy. Beat in vanilla chip mixture. Spread in bottom of pie shell. Cool remaining hot lemon mixture for at least 15 minutes , then spoon over top of cream cheese filling.
  8. Refrigerate 2 to 3 hours (uncovered or put in pie storage container). Garnish with whipped cream, almonds and berries just before serving.
Jane's "Turkey for Dessert"

This recipe is delicious, AND doubles as a centerpiece (plus, if you want to speed up the Thanksgiving dinner clean up process, why not have guests eat the décor?) This edible creation is particularly enjoyable for younger guests, but you're never too old to play with food!

Buzz words: Decorative, zany, DIY

Ingredients:

  • Oreo cookies
  • Chocolate frosting
  • Vanilla frosting (or any flavor that is white in color)
  • Peanut butter cups
  • Candy corn
  • Red food coloring
  • Malted Milk Balls
  • Toothpicks

Preparation:

  1. Place one Oreo cookie as the base. Put one teaspoon of chocolate frosting on top.
  2. lace one small peanut butter cup on its side so that the top and bottom are perpendicular to the base -- and stuck in the frosting. Place a teaspoon of chocolate frosting on the top (widest part) of the peanut butter cup.
  3. For the second cookie, stick some candy corn into the Oreo 'stuff' along one edge for the feathers -- pointy side down. They should fan out around the edge. If you have trouble doing this, you can help them stick with some white frosting.
  4. Place this second Oreo cookie (the tail) on its edge and sticking to a dab of brown frosting on the back of the peanut butter cup.
  5. Place a dab of chocolate frosting on top of the peanut butter up (for the head). Place a malted milk ball on top for the head.
  6. Take a small amount of the white frosting in a separate dish. Mix in red food coloring.
  7. Use a toothpick with the white and red colors to place eyes, and wattle. You can pinch off the small pointy top of a candy corn and dip it in brown frosting for the beak.
Sean S.'s "Vegan Rum Cake"

You definitely don't have to be a health nut to enjoy this delicacy. In fact, leave out the word "Vegan", and even the biggest carnivores at your table will be begging for seconds. Tip: Serve with a hot rum drink!

Buzz words: vegan/vegetarian, sophisticated, healthy

Ingredients:

Cake

  • 2 cups sugar
  • 1 cup coconut oil or vegan margarine
  • 3 cups flour
  • 1+ tbsp vanilla
  • 1+ tbsp baking powder
  • 4 Flax Eggs (1 Flax Egg = 1 tbsp Ground Flax seeds + 3 tbsp water)
  • 1/2 cup vegetable oil
  • 1/4 cup water
  • 1 cup rum

Glaze

  • 1 cup Cocunut Oil or Vegan Margarine
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1/4 cup water
  • 1/2 cup rum

Preparation:

Cake

  1. Preheat oven to 325 degrees F
  2. In a large bowl, mix all the cake ingredients. Using a hand or electric mixer, beat until mostly smooth.
  3. Pour mixture into a Bundt pan (spray with non-stick spray) and bake for about 45 -50 minutes.

Glaze

  1. Prepare about 5 minutes before the cake comes out of the oven - In a large sauce pan, mix all glaze ingredients over medium heat
  2. When the coconut oil or margarine is fully melted, bring to a boil and continue boiling for 3 minutes while stirring constantly
  3. Pour glaze over cake while still in Bundt pan
  4. Let the mixture soak for approximately 2 hours
  5. Turn cake over onto a plate, sift powdered sugar over the top

The Art of Communication Between EMRs

How CMR helps ACOs & CINs ensure seamless communication across the continuum

We’ve all experienced the problems that come from miscommunication. For our health care members, miscommunication can have disastrous consequences for patients, but seamless communication can help prevent health problems and improve care coordination.

TL;DR (FYI, that's internet slang for "Too Long";"Didn't Read")—there’s a new Consulting & Management engagement called Patient Access that is totally different than “Patient Access” as it is referred to in the context of the revenue cycle. Download the internal one-pager here to learn more.

So you’ve been around the firm awhile, and you know a thing or two about Patient Access. A quick search on Advisory.com indicates there are 3,886 resources tagged to this topic. Oh. That’s a lot. Well, you always could quickly skim through the 106 research briefs on the topic, or maybe watch the 232 on-demand webconferences. The point is: it’s a popular term that’s been adopted across the health care industry to with different meanings for different people in different departments.

To many at ABC and in the industry, it refers to a critical component of the revenue cycle wherein the provider registers patients, verifies their insurance, and focuses on point-of-service cash collection. That definition is right. But there is another right answer here: making sure that patients get the right care, at the right time. We have a few tools that speak to this second definition, notably HealthPost, CMR, and Audience Rx.

So why is Consulting & Management launching a new engagement centered on the second definition of patient access? Is it, as we like to say at ABC, a big “up at night issue”? Simply put, no. Or, rather, it’s not until it really, really is. Why is that? Because the symptoms of a larger patient access issue are disparate, and it isn’t obvious until all the separate small issues roll up to the CEO that these add up to something on a bigger scale.

There is no Chief Access Officer (well actually, I found one in Salesforce but this person is tied to the revenue cycle definition). So issues surrounding virtual visits, same-day access, retail partnerships, call centers, bringing in new patients, marketing, keeping old patients in-network, expanding practice hours for physicians, and scheduling don’t all roll up to the same individual until it the CEO pieces all these symptoms together.

Consulting & Management is dedicated to helping our members solve the root causes behind multiple symptoms that the organization is encountering, and do so by leveraging the resources the provider has already invested in. What should you listen for?

  • Our patients struggle to "get in" with our providers.
  • We're striving for an "open/on-demand access" model.
  • Our practices aren't efficient and/or our providers aren't productive.

And how might you know to pass a lead for Patient Access consulting instead of one of our other products, for example HealthPost? Often you will hear the member say something along the lines of, "we want to fix our processes before automating them."

Lessons from the "Blue Zones"

#TravelTuesday

According to a new book titled The Blue Zones Solution by Dan Buettner, there are 5 locations in the world that hold the secret to longevity based on their high percentages of centenarians. Advancements in health care are a key factor in longer life span, but these fairly remote points on the globe have secrets to healthy aging that date back centuries.

What’s even better, you don’t have to reside in one of these places to apply their health sustaining habits to your own lifestyle. Check it out!

1. Ikaria, Greece

Residents on this island maintain a Mediterranean diet with benefits that include brain health and defense against chronic diseases. Foods like potatoes, black eyed peas, lentils, goat’s milk, honey and moderate servings of fish make up the bulk of their daily calorie consumption.

2. Okinawa, Japan

In Okinawa, about 6.5 out of every 10,000 people live to be 100. In the entire United States, this number is only 1.73. What is their secret? Buettner cites factors such as the mild climate and laidback lifestyle, as well as “longevity foods” which include melons, shitake mushrooms, tofu, garlic, brown rice, and of course, green tea.

3. Sardinia, Italy

In Sardinia, the ratio of centenarian females to males is one to one. For the rest of the world, this ratio is five to one. A researcher discovered a unique correlation between “pastoralism” (i.e., “shepherding livestock from the mountains to the plains”) and living to 100. Though you likely won’t find an easy way to transition to a “shepherding lifestyle” in the U.S., you can incorporate things like goat’s milk and sheep’s cheese into your diet, both of which have been praised for their correlation to longevity among shepherds in Sardinia. Of course, Sardinians also maintain a physically active lifestyle out in the fresh air, and they also drink a moderate but consistent amount of red wine.

4. Loma Linda, California

A large chunk of the population in Loma Linda follows a biblical lifestyle which shuns smoking, drinking and television. Their diet consists mainly of grains, fruits, nuts and vegetables, and they drink only water. However, it was found that the pesco-vegetarians in the community (those who maintained a vegetarian diet with one moderate serving of fish per day) lived longer than those following a vegan diet. The most prominent foods in this community include avocados, salmon, nuts, beans, oatmeal, whole wheat bread and soy milk – could be worse!

5. Nicoya Peninsula, Costa Rica

According to Buettner, "The big secret of the Nicoyan diet was the 'three sisters' of Meso-American agriculture: beans, corn and squash." They also eat plenty of papayas, bananas, yams and eggs (which they incorporate into almost every meal.)

Why People Aren't Opening Your Emails

And How To Change It

This week’s blog post comes from our friends on the Digital Marketing team, who work on crafting and executing all marketing email campaign materials. Below, they’ll share tips on how to not only craft your next email, but how to get people to open and engage with your message.

So, how do you make your emails interesting and effective?

Step 1: The Subject Line

The subject line is your headline. Make sure it’s to the point. Convey your offer clearly. The subject line is like the gatekeeper of your email, so first and foremost, it should grab your attention.

We can’t be quite as cheeky as JCrew in our own corporate emails, but depending on the tone of your company, something eye-catching can go a long way. Subject lines like “Only a Few Seats Left!” for an event registration email or “3 Things Your Competitors Learned” as a follow-up to an event the receiver didn’t attend are a few attention-grabbing ideas.

Personalize

Personalizing used to mean including the name of the recipient in your copy (Good morning, Bob!) but that no longer enough. The most effective way to personalize subject lines is to first segment your audience, and then craft the subject line to reflect their exact needs and problems. Do they seem to be frequently putting up blog posts? Email them your guide to crafting social media content with a subject line “Need more Twitter followers?”

The same approach can also apply to the body of your email. Below is an example where the marketer looked at the recipient’s social media page and crafted their message to reflect their current standing and how they could help.

On the flip side, be careful to over-personalize the subject line to the point of being misleading. We’ve all received emails claiming to be from a company executive that are actually marketing ploys, and those don’t tend to win over readers.

Align Subject Line With the Email Copy: The C.U.R.V.E. Method

Once the readers open the email, they want to get exactly what they were promised in the subject line. If they don’t get what they were promised, your click rates will plummet. The Curve method was created by Alex Williams, after years of writing and testing subject lines. A good subject line must possess at least two of the five C.U.R.V.E. elements to connect with your audience.

One of the two elements of your subject line almost always should be the “R” (relevance) or the “V” (value). The other three elements are: “C” - curiosity, “U”-urgency, and “E” - emotion. Always remember that you’re talking to other humans – write what you’d want to read!

Step 2: The Email Message

Now that your audience has opened your email after you wrote a stellar subject line, how do you get them to click? Whether you are giving them something fun, sharing important news, or trying to solve a problem, here are few important steps you should follow:

Be Brief

Most recipients don’t have hours to spend reading emails. Be short and sweet and outline what your main points are. Subheadings, bullets or combination of both are always welcomed.

Establish Relevancy and Benefits

Just like the “R” in the C.U.R.V.E method for subject lines, strive to create relevancy through personalization. Let the reader know why they are being emailed, remind them of a previous conversation you had and what you can do for them.

Use Actionable Language

Including assertive words like “download,” “get,” “reserve,” “ask,” etc. show the reader exactly what they can do –or what we want them to do – after viewing your email.

It may seem silly, but sometimes emails contain so many bells and whistles that people can’t decipher the main point. Be sure to include an obvious call-to-action, such as a large, standout button or hyperlinked text in a color that doesn’t blend in with the rest of your copy. These things not only draw the eye of your reader, but also their clicks!

So You Want to Be an Advisory Board Consultant

A How To Q&A with Vanessa Athis

About Vanessa:

Vanessa Athis is a senior consultant with Advisory Board Consulting & Management, formerly known as Southwind. Vanessa started as a Dedicated Advisor with Payment Integrity Compass before making the transition to consulting Her journey represents a path that more and more employees at Advisory Board are starting to explore. But how exactly does one come to work in consulting for the Advisory Board?

Vanessa recently sat down with One Advisory to discuss her journey to her current role and give some useful advice to others interested in taking a similar path.

One Advisory Board: What was your first position at the Advisory Board?

Vanessa: Two years ago I joined the Advisory Board as a Dedicated Advisor for Payment Integrity Compass (PIC). I worked in that role for fourteen months before moving over to Southwind consulting where I am today.

OAB: When did you first realize you wanted to get into consulting?

VA: I first became interested in becoming a consultant when I was assigned a joint project at Bronx Lebanon with the Revenue Cycle Consulting team. Due to the complex nature of the engagement (we were implementing PIC while simultaneously restructuring their business office) I got the chance be on site with the member more frequently and get to know the people that worked there in a way I hadn’t with my other members. Since I liked the increased interaction and the Rev Cycle work the team was doing, I started having conversations with my manager about how I could work on more in depth projects like this.

OAB: How do you first learn about Southwind?

VA: I first heard about Southwind by reaching out to one of their internal recruiters. After a few conversations and some of my own research, I discovered the Physician Services division which does practice operations and revenue cycle consulting for medical groups. Working as a consultant there matched up perfectly with my pre-ABC background at the Emory Clinic and as PIC DA. It also matched my interest in working on more in depth engagements with members. I applied for a consulting role last year and have been working as a senior consultant ever since.

OAB: Are there any roles at ABC that can prepare you to become a consultant?

VA: I would definitely recommend becoming a DA, especially if you get to support a technology. That’s because being a DA teaches you how to use reporting which is key in consulting. It also teaches you what metrics are important to a member’s success. I look forward to recommending PIC and other ABC PT products to my future Southwind clients. Because of the reporting experience I got working as a PIC DA, I know what great results look like.

OAB: What is your day to day like?

VA: I’m face to face with members every day unless it’s a travel day. What my day actually looks like really depends on the engagement I’m working on. As a new consultant, you often find yourself working with middle and front line level staff that can be very resistant to change. This means getting good a change management is huge.

Currently, I’m working with front desk staff and management of a medical practices and am constantly working to instill confidence in the policies we’re implementing by explaining exactly what’s happening at their practice, what their goals are, what the changes we’re making will do to help. It’s so satisfying to see the impact we get to make on a daily basis.

OAB: What do you like about working at Southwind?

VA: Southwind is a close knit group which I’m thrilled to be a part of. Because we’re on the road with each other all the time, we develop strong bonds which can help when working on engagements. This is especially true at the beginning of engagements, where clients tend to see you as an outsider. It’s then that being able to lean on your fellow consultants is great.

OAB: If you were to give one piece of advice to debating if consulting is for them, what would it be?

VA: Figure out if you’re a people person. As a DA you don’t HAVE to be one because you don’t have to spend long amounts of time with people. But at Southwind you’re onsite all day and members pay a lot for your advice. You have to have your game face on, but the relationships you build are wonderful. You’re always dealing with new people and new challenges. You eventually get close enough to people that you learn from them just as much as you teach them. That’s what gets me excited to go to work every day.

Jordan’s “Butternut Squash Gratin” (BSG)

This recipe is for: The chef who wants to offer a festive take on a classic dish! This side dish is sure to warm the hearts and bellies of members of all ages.

Buzz words: Autumn-appropriate, comfort food, third helpings

Ingredients:

  • 1 (3-lb.) butternut squash
  • 1 (3-lb.) spaghetti squash
  • 2 tablespoons butter, melted
  • 1 cup firmly packed light brown sugar, divided
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground nutmeg
  • 3 cups whipping cream
  • 5 large Yukon gold potatoes (about 2 1/2 lb.)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon freshly ground pepper
  • 4 cups (16 oz.) freshly shredded fontina cheese

Preparation:

  1. Preheat oven to 450°. Cut butternut and spaghetti squash in half lengthwise; remove seeds. Place squash, cut sides up, in a lightly greased 17 x 12-inch jelly-roll pan. Drizzle with butter, and sprinkle with 1/2 cup brown sugar. Bake 40 minutes or until tender. Cool 20 minutes.
  2. Using a fork, scrape inside of spaghetti squash to remove spaghetti-like strands, and place in a large bowl. Scoop pulp from butternut squash; coarsely chop pulp, and toss with spaghetti squash.
  3. Stir together cinnamon, nutmeg, and remaining 1/2 cup brown sugar.
  4. Cook cream in a heavy nonaluminum saucepan over medium heat, stirring often, for 5 minutes or just until it begins to steam (do not boil); remove from heat.
  5. Using a mandoline or sharp knife, cut potatoes into 1/8-inch-thick slices.
  6. Arrange one-fourth of potato slices in a thin layer on bottom of a buttered 13 x 9-inch baking dish. Spoon one-third of squash mixture over potatoes (squash layer should be about 1/4-inch thick); sprinkle with 1/4 tsp. salt, 1/4 tsp. pepper, 1 cup fontina cheese, and 3/4 cup hot cream. Repeat layers twice, sprinkling one-third of sugar mixture over each of second and third squash layers. (Do not sprinkle sugar mixture over first squash layer.) Top with remaining potato slices, 1/4 tsp. salt, and 1/4 tsp. pepper. Gently press layers down with back of a spoon. Sprinkle top with remaining 1 cup cheese and 3/4 cup hot cream; sprinkle with remaining brown sugar mixture. Place baking dish on an aluminum foil-lined baking sheet.
  7. Bake, covered with foil, at 450° for 1 hour; uncover and bake 25 more minutes or until golden brown and potatoes are tender. Cool on a wire rack 20 minutes before serving.

OAB 201 Skills

Active Listening Tips to Surface Member Intel

The #1 question lead passers ask during OAB training sessions tends to be some iteration of “but I’m not in Sales and my role isn’t commercial, so how do I pass leads?”

Late last year, I set out to answer this question for lead passers. With the help of the Corporate Executive Board, I identified two active listening tactics. These tactics can be used alone or in tandem. The goal is to surface member intel and service opportunities. If the tactics help you identify a lead – all the better!


1. Leading with Quality Assurance

What: taking a QA approach with current members

Why: Understanding how your member views ABC and their current membership services can help uncover service opportunities and help see ABC from the member’s point of view. Plus, if you do want to pass a lead this tactic helps you introduce the new product in meaningful and impactful way.

How: Ask questions! It is ok to talk about things other than your product or topic area. It is also ok if you don’t have all the answers (gasp!... that’s what OAB is for).

  • How would you describe ABC to a colleague?
  • What membership services are most important to you in your role? Why?
  • How do you measure success of membership?

2. The Jobs and Outcomes Model

What: Jobs are defined as part of your member’s workflow that represent the tasks or activities members need or want accomplished. Members eventually “hire” products to fulfill jobs, just like they would hire an additional member of their staff. Each job has a clear outcome. Jobs are:

  • Benefit focused
  • Member defined
  • Measurable
  • Product agnostic
  • Prescriptive and action oriented

Why: Having a conversation with your member through the Jobs and Outcomes model helps steer the conversation away from specific products toward solutions.

Here’s an example:

If you’re speaking to a member and they tell you that right no, their biggest priority is to focus on referrals in their market. That’s the job they’re working on. If you respond with something to the effect of “Crimson Market Advantage is a performance technology ABC offers that blah blah blah blah…” the member won’t understand. What they will understand is if you lose the ABC lingo and tell them how we can help them get to their desired outcome. Now you’ve got their attention.

How: You guessed it…ask questions!

  • What would happen if you didn’t achieve your desired outcome?
  • If you had to rank the different project you’re working on right now, which would come first? Last?
  • If you found yourself with a day of zero meetings, what’s the first problem you’d set out to solve for the hospital/health system?

"The first job is to solidify the voice of the customer. That's where everything has to start. What this does is allow us to walk in our customers' shows to see what they do day-in and day-out. We learn things we never knew."
- Marian Powell, SVP Sales/Marketing/Product Management/E-Business , GE Capital Fleet Services

Book Review: The Moment of Clarity

Applying Social Science to Better Serve Our Members and Motivate Each Other

Is yoga a sport?

This is my favorite case study question highlighted in The Moment of Clarity: Using the Human Sciences to Solve Your Toughest Business Problems by Christian Madsbjerg and Mikkel Rasmussen. Posed by a major sporting goods company executive in the early 2000s, his company was losing market share for reasons he could not articulate.

This question was a game changer because it challenged the core assumption the entire industry was operating under at the time: sports products are produced to help athletes win. If yoga was a sport, it meant consumers were now engaging in sports, not primarily for competition, but for enjoyment, health, and appearance. This was a massive shift in understanding the industry’s consumer base and by the 2012 over half the sporting goods market was made up of fitness apparel geared at noncompetitive “sports” like yoga and jogging (Lululemon anyone?).

The Moment of Clarity as an easy read that raises some interesting ideas about how we should approach problem solving when we find ourselves in a fog (defined by the book as a time where we know something is wrong, but can’t quite define what the problem is). Too often, it argues we get stuck in approaching such problems with traditional assumptions like that people are rational actors, tomorrow will look the same as today, and our hypotheses are objective and nonbiased. Problems this vague are better solved through the use of social science. Christian and Mikkel call this problem solving technique sensemaking and it posits that by studying the human experience of those we are trying to influence, we can gain insight that can set our business on clear pathway forward.

How Can You Apply This to Your Role at ABC?

When it comes to solving problems we face every day at ABC, this book has a lot to offer. In my own job, it has helped me rethink how I influence the team of MAs I work with daily. I realized I too often assume monetary incentives will drive my desired outcomes. If I need X visits by the end of the week, I’ll offer them each $100 if they hit that number. However, as soon as I started applying some of the sensemaking techniques, I gained a clearer understanding of what the experience of being a recent college grad in an intro sales job is like. This helped me realize that other methods can be equally, if not more effective motivators such as public recognition or unique opportunities for career pathing.

Sensemaking can help us better understand our members as well. Rather than jumping to default thinking like, “more CNOs will buy this membership if I can find new way to bundle it”, take time to listen to what they say and, if possible, talk with someone about what their day to day is like. What is it LIKE being a leader of a team of nurses? What are their daily challenges? How do they fit into the larger context of a CNO’s life? If we can gain a better understand of our members through this process outlined in The Moment of Clarity, we’ll be in a much better position to serve their needs going forward. For that reason, I highly recommend checking it out.

Who Gets the Conference Room?

One Advisory Proposes a Few Rules for Meeting Rooms

The Wall Street Journal recently published an article titled “New Office Flashpoint: Who Gets the Conference Room?”. Given the renovation of the DC office and then huge uptick of team and conference rooms, the article caught my eye. According to the article, upwards of 40% of employees waste up to 30 minutes per day looking for meeting space.

I know those 30, very frustrating, minutes well.

After I got over the fact that every meeting at Skull Candy—a Park City, Utah, maker of wireless headphones—seems to involve a case of PBR, I started to think about ways to improve the Advisory Board’s use of our limited conference rooms.

1. Transparency

All team rooms have dry erase markers. Use them to save your colleagues some time. When you enter a conference room (that isn’t on Meeting Room Manager), take 15 seconds to write when your meeting will be over on the window next to the door. If it’s 1:55 and I’m looking for a team room, I can’t tell if your meeting or call will be done at 2:00 or 2:30. If I know it’s 2:00, I can wait until you’re done. If it’s 2:30, I can continue my search.

2. Keep an Eye on the Clock

See those people milling around outside the conference room you reserved until 12:00? Is it 12:04? You have overstayed your reservation and it’s time to let others have the room. I know that those last three minutes of your meeting are super important, which is exactly why the ball won’t get dropped if you finish the discussion in the hallway or over email. When you overstay your reservation, you create a domino effect of lateness. This is not Nam. There are rules.

3. Give Directions

Meeting with people from a different floor or a different office? Give them directions! It’s great that you walk by Heathrow, or NoMa, or Vermont every day but I have no idea where it is. How many times have you seen me wandering around your floor looking for Danny? Just give me a hint. “Take a left at the common room and it’s the first room on the right,” or “it’s two rooms down from the men’s bathroom.” Anything helps.

True North: Work/Life Balance and Advice

An Interview with Ford Koles

About Ford:

Ford Koles is the Executive Director of the Educational Services team and has been one of the most respected ABC experts on health care strategy and economics over his 22 years at the firm.

On August 22nd, Ford sat down with One Advisory to discuss “True North,” work/life balance, and his advice for younger staff looking to make an impact on members.

One Advisory Board: What Does True North Mean to You?

Ford Koles: True North is the principle differentiator between us and anyone else. Whether that be an association, a consulting firm, SG2, Epic, you name it. Why is our information better? Because we hold ourselves to that higher standard: magnetic north vs. True North is the juxtaposition between common wisdom and true insight. Without True North, any best practice is just a cool and funky new idea. Like the ice bucket challenge. Those things are new and fun but not part of any True North. What we do is anchor everything we do in a bigger picture. When I worked for consulting firms, what we wanted to do was find the hot new idea and that’s what we wanted to sell. That was fun for a while, but stepping back I don’t know that what I was selling was all that impactful. True North, in that sense, is everything for me. If I didn’t have that I think I would feel a lot more like a hamster in a wheel.

OAB: When you’ve had a long week with a lot of travel or are feeling frustrated, how do you keep your compass set?

FK: I maintain a very good work life balance. I never get down to zero. I know that if I have a tough time on the home front, I have the Advisory Board. The fact that I like my job and that a lot of people don’t like their job in the rest of the world, it helps balance me. And the opposite is true. If I have a tough day or an executive is rude to me, then I go home and play with my kids.It works both ways and I think people forget that.

OAB: Any advice for staff earlier in their careers who are looking to make a difference for their members?

FK: It’s all about finding your gift. David Bradley gave me the following four-part advice:

  1. Ask yourself, when were you at your happiest? What were you doing?
  2. Don’t say you want to work in finance or healthcare. Those are nouns. Get to the verb. What’s the verb, not the noun? What is it you want to do? Do you want to sell? To renew? To manage?
  3. You’re in an MBA project group and everyone is working to get a job done. And you go around the table and look at the tasks and you say, “That’s the job I want to do,” and all your friends say, “That’s the job you should do.” What is that thing?
  4. Make sure your ladder is leaning up against the right building. Everyone at ABC is so type A that if they see a ladder in front of them, they are going to climb their way to the top of it. Then you find yourself at the top of the ladder and wonder how you got up there. So make sure your ladder is leaning up against the right building before you start climbing.

In a day to day sense, I try really hard to be a good listener. When you sit with a member executive, listen to them. Really listen. What’s the problem? We spend way too much of our time with an agenda. But I find I’m doing my best when I’m really listening.

If you remember one sentence from this blog post, remember this: PIC-Forecaster enables members to understand the financial impact of delivering value-based care and capitalize on their care transformation efforts.

Everyone is focused on cutting costs and improving efficiency, but at the end of the day the result is reduced volume. The Mid-Cycle

The Final Frontier of Optimizing Revenue

With the amount of pressure hospitals are under with the increase in value-based purchasing and the transition to ICD-10, the Revenue Cycle team has spent the last year trying to answer a difficult question: What is the biggest potential area of impact within the revenue cycle that will allow hospitals to fund these changes?

Historically, revenue cycles have looked at back office operations – how quickly can we get paid what we’re owed? More recently, they’ve focused on the front end – how can we increase collections from patients as they present? But our research shows that the area we’ve begun to term the “mid-cycle” – the moment the physician begins to documents what happens in the operating room to the time a bill is coded and turned over for collection – has been the biggest inefficiency for the hospital revenue cycle in the last few years, and is really the final frontier of optimizing revenue.

…but what does it mean to optimize the rev cycle? It boils down to 3 things:

  1. Getting paid every dollar that a hospital is owed for the services provided
  2. Doing so as quickly and as efficiently as possible
  3. Ensuring that no one ever takes it back

And that’s where ROC comes in. ROC allows hospitals the get a complete picture of their documentation and coding performance. It shines a light on hospitals’ biggest opportunities so they can improve revenue capture, prevent take backs, and improve quality measures.

So how can you tell when an organization could benefit from ROC? Start by asking these questions:

  • Do you have visibility into the performance of your mid-cycle?
  • Do you feel like you have the financial and revenue data in your mid-cycle to understand the areas in which you need to improve?
  • Do you feel like you know how to measure and get ROI from that group?

Advisory Board Tradeshow Strategy

I recently attended a tradeshow with over 230,000 square feet of exhibit space. As I walked through the aisles, carefully avoiding the magicians, I was surprised by how simple it was for me to decide which booths were worth stopping in and which I was happy to skip. I never made the decision based on the product or service the booth was promoting, as this wasn’t necessarily obvious at first glance, but rather the initial greeting I received from the booth staff and the overall look and feel of the booth. The Advisory Board has been exhibiting at tradeshows for six years now. It is important for us to have a strong presence at the major health care tradeshows, including HIMSS, AMGA, ASHHRA, SHSMD, and IHI, as many of our members frequent health care tradeshow floors.

As a company we are working to improve our overall tradeshow presence in the following ways:

  1. Maximize quality foot traffic by vetting attendee lists beforehand and reaching out with personal invitations to meet: The tradeshow floor can be an overwhelming experience. When you schedule time for someone to come by your booth, you have created structure for them in an otherwise hectic day. It is also easier for us to understand the ROI of a specific show by tracking the outcome of these in-booth meetings.
  2. Creating a consistent booth theme: Our goal across the next 12 months is for our booth to have an overall brand, consistent with ABC’s firm values, that is immediately recognizable on a tradeshow floor. This shift in focus from product to firm will help generate onsite interest and produce quality leads.
  3. Equipping our onsite staff to be our brand ambassadors and create meaningful connections with members and prospects: Exhibiting is expensive, and we only get to be on the tradeshow floor for about 12 hours per show. The goal of a tradeshow floor interaction is not to close a sale; rather, it is to generate interest in our offerings and make a great first impression. It is important for all booth staff to understand how to maximize the precious time we have by greeting each attendee, assessing their needs, and setting up immediate next steps.
  4. Improve the use of in-booth interactive technology: We want to think beyond the standard demo stations we have in our tradeshow booth to technology that allows attendees to interact with our product offerings on their own. Installing interactive kiosks is our next step in improving our overall tradeshow presence.

Funky Tomatoes: Cooking Tips for Busy ABCers

An interview with Rivka Friedman

About Rivka:

Rivka is a Senior Consultant with the Medical Group Strategy Council research team and has been with the Advisory Board for six and half years. She lives and works in Washington, D.C.

Rivka is well known for her passion for food and cooking. She is regularly interviewed by area media and maintains her popular blog: Not Derby Pie. You can also follow Rivka on Twitter @notderbypie.

One Advisory Board sat down with Rivka in July to learn more about her love of food and pick up some tips for cooking healthy, affordable meals, while juggling the demands of working at the Advisory Board. Edited excerpts below.

One Advisory Board: Can you tell us a little bit about what got you interested in food and cooking?

Rivka Friedman: I grew up watching my mom cook, and then standing on a stool next to her, and then helping her, and then at some point – I think in sixth grade –I said, “I would like to be in charge of all my own lunches.” My parents were not great lunch makers, and I never enjoyed my lunch. So I just started making my own lunch, and that was the beginning of the end.

OAB: Given how busy everybody is at the Advisory Board, it can be difficult to find time to cook, especially on week days, how do you find the time?

RF: Half my cooking is done on the weekend. Generally, I’ll roast two pans of vegetables, make a pot of grains, and then get some sort of cheese or other protein, and I’ll mix and match the elements across the week. So, one day I am eating wheatberries with eggplant and feta, another day I’m eating wheatberries with cucumber and soy dressing, a third day I’m eating rice with tomatoes and goat cheese, whatever it is. The combinations are endless, and it’s an easy way to squeeze in homemade cooking when time is of the essence.

OAB: Do you have any tips for shopping – places you often shop, what to look for in produce or meat, or how to find the best prices?

RF: I love the Foggy Bottom Farmer’s Market. It’s on Wednesday’s, from 3-7, so it’s perfectly timed for the end of the work day. My other big tip for getting good food at low cost is to look for “seconds.” My favorite tomato grower at the Dupont Farmer’s Market on Sundays is named Toigo, and they have fantastic tomatoes that are like $4 a pound – not cheap – but they have a secret little crate underneath the table that has the misshapen ones, the ones with maybe a soft spot, and those are like 99 cents a pound, which is cheaper than the grocery store. They’re great for tomato sauce or great for gazpacho – and many farmers also offer second peaches and plums, too, so “seconds” are the way to go.

OAB: What are three culinary skills everyone young professional should master?

RF: 1) How to use a knife. I think people think a blunt knife is good because it won’t cut you as hard. But actually, a sharp knife is good because it won’t slip. There are a lot of great knife skills classes in the area that will make you feel more confident when you cut.

2) How to make an egg! An egg goes on everything. I think people under utilize the “put an egg on it” mentality for dinner. Learn how to make it consistently every time and you can pretty much put it on whatever’s in your fridge and have dinner.

3) I think people focus a lot on how to prepare a good cut of meat. I would put my own dollars and time and energy into how to make a perfect pan of vegetables...getting really good at cooking vegetables will make what you eat cheaper and more delicious.

OAB: What are your favorite resources for recipes, inspiration, and advice?

RF: If you’re looking for a book to read, I highly recommend An Everlasting Meal, by Tamar Adler. A lot of the tips I shared are actually things she recommends in that book. In terms of blogs, I obviously have my own blog, but I would recommend Food 52. It is a curated, crowd sourced website. So, it’s not just the Yelp of recipes, it’s the Yelp but then there are editors behind the site who are choosing “this one’s worth making, this one maybe isn’t worth making.” And so the cream rises to the top on Food 52, great resource.
